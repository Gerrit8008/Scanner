2. Network Security

What is Network Security?

Network security focuses on protecting the infrastructure and connections that allow systems to communicate. This includes analyzing open ports (potential entry points for attackers), gateway configurations, and firewall settings. Proper network security prevents unauthorized access to your systems, protects against data breaches, and helps maintain operational continuity. Attackers often use automated tools to scan networks for vulnerable entry points, making this an essential security layer.

3. Web Security

What is Web Security?

Web security focuses on protecting your websites, web applications, and the data they handle. This includes analyzing your SSL/TLS certificates (which encrypt communications), HTTP security headers (which protect against common web attacks), and content management systems for vulnerabilities. Web attacks are among the most common security threats organizations face, as websites are publicly accessible and often connected to valuable internal resources or customer data. Strong web security is particularly critical for organizations that process sensitive information or transactions online.

4. Email Security

What is Email Security?

Email security focuses on protecting your email communications from spoofing, phishing, and other attacks. This includes analyzing SPF, DKIM, and DMARC records, which are DNS records that verify email senders and prevent impersonation. Email is one of the most common attack vectors, with phishing attacks accounting for a significant percentage of security breaches. Proper email authentication helps protect your brand reputation by preventing attackers from sending emails that appear to come from your domain, while also improving email deliverability by establishing your domain as a legitimate sender.

5. Security Recommendations & Action Plan

Why Are Recommendations Important?

Recommendations provide actionable steps to address the security issues identified in your scan. These are prioritized by risk level, allowing you to focus on the most critical vulnerabilities first. Following these recommendations will significantly improve your security posture and reduce your organization's risk of a breach. The timeline recommendations help you develop a structured remediation plan suitable for your resources and risk tolerance.

Next Steps: Based on our scan results, we recommend the following actions to improve your security posture, ordered by priority:
Priority Issue Severity Action Timeframe
1 Missing DMARC record Critical Implement DMARC with a 'quarantine' or 'reject' policy Immediate
2 Port 3389 (RDP) exposed Critical Close RDP port or implement VPN for remote access Immediate
3 WordPress outdated (version 5.8.3) High Update WordPress to the latest version (6.5) Within 1 week
4 SSL Certificate expiring soon High Renew SSL certificate before expiration Within 1 week
5 Missing security headers High Implement recommended security headers (CSP, HSTS) Within 2 weeks

Detailed Recommendations

  • Implement DMARC: Set up a DMARC record with at least a "quarantine" policy to protect against email spoofing. This should be connected to monitoring to track potential abuse of your domain.
  • Secure Remote Access: Close port 3389 (RDP) or implement a VPN solution for secure remote access. RDP exposed directly to the internet is a common attack vector.
  • Update WordPress: Update your WordPress installation to the latest version and implement automatic updates for security patches. Remove any unused plugins and themes.
  • Renew SSL Certificate: Schedule the renewal of your SSL certificate before its expiration date. Consider implementing auto-renewal to avoid future expirations.
  • Implement Security Headers: Configure your web server to include critical security headers such as Content-Security-Policy, Strict-Transport-Security, and Referrer-Policy.
  • Implement DKIM Signing: Set up DKIM for your domain to add cryptographic authentication for your outgoing emails.
  • Review Firewall Rules: Audit your firewall configuration to ensure it follows the principle of least privilege, allowing only necessary traffic.

Threat scenarios illustrate how the vulnerabilities identified in your scan could be exploited by attackers in real-world situations. These scenarios help translate technical findings into business risks, making it easier to understand the potential impact on your organization. By understanding these scenarios, you can better prioritize security investments and communicate risks to stakeholders who may not have technical backgrounds. Each scenario includes an impact and likelihood assessment to help with risk management decisions.

Remote Desktop Brute Force Attack

With Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP) exposed on port 3389, attackers could attempt brute force password attacks to gain unauthorized access to your systems. Once access is gained, attackers could move laterally through your network, exfiltrate data, or deploy ransomware.

Impact: Critical Likelihood: Medium
